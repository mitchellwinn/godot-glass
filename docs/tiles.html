The Tiles Editor

Build a whole room in 3D by stamping little pictures from a tile sheet onto the floor and walls — then dress it up with furniture, walls, columns and stairs, and even pick the sound your footsteps make. No coding, ever. If you've used Sprytile or Crocotile, this will feel like home.

Want a printable copy? Open this page in any web browser, then choose Print and "Save as PDF."

Think of the Tiles editor as a 3D coloring book. You have a sheet of tile pictures (a single image full of little squares — bricks, grass, wood, water) and a 3D grid floating in space. You pick a square off the sheet and "stamp" it onto the grid to build floors, walls and ceilings. Then you sprinkle in furniture and props, snap in modular walls and stairs, and tell the game where characters can walk. Everything happens live — what you see while you build is exactly what shows up in the game.

Before anything appears: turn the editor on

The Tiles editor is shy. It stays completely hidden until you tell the game which room you're building. The moment you do, its panel and its glowing 3D grid pop into view. The moment you click away, they vanish.

  1. Add a new Tiled Map to your scene. In the scene you want to build, use Add Child, then type Tiled Map in the search box and add it. This is the "canvas" your whole room lives on.
    Exact name to type

    In the search box, the canvas is listed under the single searchable name MahouTiledMap (all one word, no spaces). That's just the behind-the-scenes label — you'll always think of it as your room canvas.

  2. Click on it to select it. As soon as it's highlighted, the editor wakes up.
    What you'll see

    A tall control panel slides into the dock on the right side of your screen, and a glowing white grid appears floating in the 3D view, centered on your canvas. That grid is the surface you'll be stamping onto. Click away to a different thing and both disappear — your work is safe, it's just hidden.

  3. Give it a tile sheet (if it doesn't have one). Your canvas needs a sheet of tile pictures to stamp from. You can drop one in from the Inspector, or pick one from the Tileset dropdown on the panel (covered below). The panel automatically borrows whatever sheet and tile size your canvas already has.

Getting to know the panel

The panel is your whole control center. From top to bottom it has: a row of mode buttons, an axis row that picks floor-vs-wall, and then a set of tabs that change depending on what you're doing. Let's walk through every single control so nothing is a mystery.

The mode buttons (top row)

This top strip is always there. Each button is one "thing you're doing right now." Only one is active at a time, and the matching tab below opens automatically. Each button also has a one-key shortcut shown in brackets.

The eight modes
Paint B
The default. Stamps your chosen tile picture onto the grid. This is how you build floors, walls and ceilings.
Erase E
Rubs tiles out. Click or drag over tiles to remove them from the layer you're on.
Fill F
Floods a whole rectangle at once. Click one corner, drag to the far corner, let go — the whole box fills with your tile (repeating it to cover the area). Great for big floors.
Vertex V
Lets you grab the actual corner points of your tile surface and nudge them around, so a flat surface can be tilted or warped. For fine, sculptural tweaks.
Shape Q
Drops a ready-made many-sided column (a cylinder) at the spot you click, with every side wrapped in your chosen tile picture. Perfect for pillars and round towers.
Object O
Places furniture and props — chairs, plants, crates — anywhere you like, not locked to the grid. Opens the Objects tab.
Footstep T
Paints what sound a character's feet make on each floor tile — wood, dirt, water, and so on. Opens the Footstep tab.
Architecture A
Snaps in modular building pieces — walls, columns, stairs — that lock neatly to the grid and rotate in quarter-turns. Opens the Architecture tab.

To the right of those buttons, after a little divider, are two more things:

Also on the top row
Grid G
Turns the glowing 3D grid on or off. On by default. Toggle it off when you want a clean look at your room; toggle it back on to keep stamping precisely. The G key does the same thing.
Mode label
A little read-only word — PAINT, ERASE, FILL, VERTEX, SHAPE, OBJECT, FOOTSTEP, or ARCH — that always tells you which mode you're in. It even changes color per mode (green for paint, red for erase, and so on) so you can tell at a glance.

The axis row — floor or wall?

This is the most important idea in the whole editor. Your stamps land on a flat work surface floating in space, and this row decides whether that surface is lying flat like a floor or standing up like a wall.

The three buttons are labelled XZ, XY and YZ. Don't let those letters intimidate you — they're just behind-the-scenes shorthand for the three surfaces: the floor, the wall in front of you, and the wall to your side. Once you know which is which (below), you can ignore the letters entirely.

Axis controls
Axis: label
Just a label that says "Axis:". Read-only.
XZ 1
Forces the work surface to lie flat — this is your floor or ceiling. Click it, or press 1. Press again to release the lock and let the editor guess the surface from how your camera is pointed.
XY 2
Stands the surface up as a front or back wall (the kind you face head-on). Click it, or press 2.
YZ 3
Stands the surface up as a side wall (the kind that runs away from you to the left or right). Click it, or press 3.
Plane readout
After a divider, a little read-only readout like "XZ | Y=0.00". The first part tells you which surface is active. The number after the bar is simply how far up, in, or over the surface currently sits — it's just a depth measurement, and the bigger the number the further along it is. Watch it change as you switch surfaces or push the plane deeper. (More on pushing the plane below.)
Tip

If you don't lock an axis, the editor picks one for you based on your view: look straight down and it gives you a floor; look at a wall straight on and it gives you that wall. Locking with 1/2/3 is for when you want to keep painting one surface while spinning the camera around.

The tabs

Below the axis row is a set of tabs: Tiles, Objects, Footstep, and Architecture. You almost never click these directly — picking a mode opens the right tab for you. Tile-stamping modes (Paint, Erase, Fill, Vertex, Shape) all use the Tiles tab; Object mode opens Objects; Footstep mode opens Footstep; Architecture mode opens Architecture. You can click a tab by hand if you like.

The Tiles tab — your stamping workbench

This is where you'll spend most of your time. It holds your layers, your stamp settings, and the all-important tile sheet at the bottom.

Layer row — stacking tiles without a fight

You get four layers, numbered 0, 1, 2, 3. Think of them as four sheets of clear plastic stacked on the same spot. A higher layer sits a hair in front of the one below so two tiles in the same place never flicker or fight each other. Each layer has its own tint color (white, red, green, blue) so you can tell them apart.

Layer row
Layer:
A label. Read-only.
0 / 1 / 2 / 3
Pick which layer you're painting and erasing on. Only one is active. Whatever you stamp or rub out only touches this layer — so you can put a rug (layer 1) on top of a floor (layer 0) and erase the rug without disturbing the floor.
Overlay
A toggle, after a divider. Turn it on and every tile gets tinted by its layer's color, so you can instantly see which tiles live on which layer. Turn it off to see your true colors again.

Actions row — the stamp's pose and the cleanup buttons

Actions row
Rot R
Spins your stamp a quarter-turn each press, cycling through all four directions. Affects every tile you stamp afterward. Click it or press R.
FlipH H
Mirrors the stamp left-to-right. A toggle. Click it or press H.
FlipV J
Mirrors the stamp top-to-bottom. A toggle. Click it or press J. (On walls the editor quietly corrects this flip for you so it always looks right-side-up.)
Clear
Wipes out every tile on the layer you're currently on — a clean slate for that layer only. Don't panic if you misclick: one undo brings it all back.
Nav
Marks out where characters can walk based on your floor tiles (and any stairs you've placed). Lay your floors and stairs first, then click Nav — it reads what's already there, so clicking it on an empty room (before you've painted any floor) simply finds nothing to mark and does nothing. Re-click it whenever you add or remove floors.
Collision
Rebuilds the solid ground — the invisible surfaces that stop characters from falling through floors or walking through walls. Unlike Nav, you can click this any time; it just rebuilds from whatever exists right now. Click it to refresh after big changes. (It also refreshes on its own whenever you save the scene.)

Offset row — nudging a stamp off the grid

Sometimes a tile needs to sit a few pixels off the neat grid lines. These three spinners shift your stamp by a tiny amount. Each goes from −16 to +16, one pixel at a time, and you'll see the change live in the ghost preview under your mouse.

Offset row
Offset:
A label. Read-only.
H:
Slides the stamp sideways on its surface.
V:
Slides the stamp up or down on its surface.
D:
Pushes the stamp slightly in front of or behind its surface (depth) — handy to lift one tile just off the wall so it reads as separate.

Snap row — how finely things move

Snap row
Snap:
A label. Read-only.
Snap amount (dropdown)
How big each "step" is when you're dragging corner points in Vertex mode. Choose from coarse to ultra-fine: 16px, 8px, 4px, 2px, or 1px. Bigger steps keep things tidy; smaller steps let you fine-tune. Each choice in the dropdown also shows a second number in brackets — its size in the game's world units — so you'll see "16px (0.5)", "8px (0.25)", "4px (0.125)", "2px (0.0625)" and "1px (0.03125)". Don't worry about that second number; the px label is all you need to pick by.

Lock row — point the work surface exactly

If you know the exact spot you want your stamping surface to sit, you can type it in here instead of eyeballing it.

Lock row
Lock:
A label. Read-only.
X: / Y: / Z:
Type the exact position you want to lock onto. Each goes from −100 to +100 in half steps.
Go
Applies what you typed: it snaps your current work surface to that depth. (It uses whichever of the three numbers matches your active surface direction.)

The hold-S reminder

Right under the lock row is a little hint that reads "Hold S = Snap to Tile | Release = Lock." It's reminding you of the handiest trick in the editor — covered fully in the shortcuts section. Hold S, hover near a tile you've already placed, and your work surface jumps to line up with it perfectly. Let go to commit.

Important

Hold S works only while painting, erasing, or filling tiles (the three tile-stamping modes). In Shape, Vertex, Object and Architecture modes, pressing S does nothing — so don't expect the yellow snap marker to appear there.

Shape controls (only when you're in Shape mode)

When you switch to Shape mode, three extra settings appear here to describe the column you're about to drop.

Shape controls
Sides:
How many flat faces the column has, from 3 (a triangle) up to 32 (smooth and round). Default is 8.
Radius:
How wide the column is, from 0.5 up to 10. Default is 1.
Height:
How tall the column is, from 0.5 up to 20. Default is 2. Every side gets wrapped in your selected tile picture.

The tile sheet — your palette

At the bottom of the Tiles tab is the star of the show: the actual sheet of tile pictures you stamp from.

Tile sheet area
Tileset (dropdown)
Picks which sheet of tile pictures you're using. The first choice, "(From Inspector)", uses whatever sheet your canvas already has. The rest are sheets the editor found for you in your project's room art folders. Pick one to load it as your palette.
Zoom number
A read-only label like "2x" telling you how zoomed-in the palette is.
Zoom slider
Drag to make the palette grid bigger or smaller, from 1x up to 8x. Default is 2x. Zoom in when the squares are tiny and hard to click.
Tile info
A read-only readout showing exactly which square you've picked, like "Tile: (3, 5)". If you've grabbed a block of several squares at once, it tacks the block's size onto the end in brackets — so a three-wide, two-tall block reads "Tile: (3, 5) [3x2]". The bracketed "[WxH]" just means "this many squares wide by this many tall," so you'll recognize it the moment you drag across more than one square.
The palette grid itself
Your sheet of tile pictures, with grid lines drawn over it and a blue highlight on your pick. Click a square to choose one tile. Click and drag across squares to grab a whole rectangular block — perfect for stamping a big repeating pattern in one go.

The Objects tab — furniture and props

Objects are free-roaming decorations — chairs, plants, lamps, crates. Unlike tiles, they can sit anywhere, not just on grid lines. This tab has two sub-modes: Paint (drop new props) and Select (move and tweak the ones already there).

Objects tab controls
Paint / Select sub-mode
Two buttons up top. Paint lets you drop and erase props. Select lets you pick existing props to move, rotate or delete. Switching to Paint clears whatever you had selected.
Category (dropdown)
The broad group of props to choose from — for example Interiors (indoor furniture) or Exteriors (outdoor stuff). Picking a category refills the prop list below.

Why don't I see my own folder? The choices here are exactly the prop folders your project was set up to use — nothing more. If you've made a brand-new folder of furniture and it isn't in this list, that's why: the list only shows the project's known prop folders, so a new folder has to be added to the project's setup before it appears here.
Object (dropdown)
The specific prop you want to place. The first choice is "(None)". Pick one, then click in the 3D view to drop it in.
Snap (checkbox, on)
When ticked, props click neatly onto the grid as you place and drag them — and that grid is the very same one set by Grid Size over in the Architecture tab (your building-block grid), not your tile grid. So if Grid Size is 0.5, props snap to a half-step grid; make Grid Size finer or coarser to change how tightly props snap. The same snapping also applies when you drag a prop to move it in Select sub-mode (covered below) — moves jump to that grid too. Untick this box to drop or move a prop at the exact spot under your mouse, free of any grid.
Rot:
How much each new prop is turned when you place it, from 0° to 350° in 10° steps. Set the angle before you click. This only aims props you're about to drop — it does nothing to props already on the floor. (To re-aim a prop that's already there, select it and use the Rotation: control in the Selection section instead.)
Brush:
The size of your eraser, from 1 to 10. With a bigger brush, a right-click sweeps away every prop in a wider circle at once. This only changes how wide right-click erasing reaches — it has no effect on placing props, so a bigger brush will not drop several props at once. Left-click always places just one prop at a time.
Hint line
A read-only reminder of what your clicks do right now. In Paint it says "LMB: Place | RMB: Erase." In Select it reminds you about clicking, Shift, the rotate keys, randomize, and delete.

When you're in Select sub-mode and you've clicked a prop, a small Selection section appears with extra controls for that exact prop:

Selection controls (appear when a prop is selected)
Selection:
A header that shows up only when something's selected.
Rotation:
Re-aims the prop you've already selected to an exact turn, 0° to 350° in 10° steps. Don't mix this up with Rot: up in the Paint settings — that one only aims props you're about to place, while this one turns the one sitting on the floor right now. (That's why nudging "Rot:" never moves a prop that's already down — you need this control for that.)
Y Offset:
Raise or lower the selected prop, from −10 to +10 in tenths. Lift a lamp off the floor or sink a rug slightly into it.
Convex Collision (checkbox, on) — how solid the prop is
Controls the shape of the prop's solid ground — the invisible barrier characters bump into. On (the default) wraps the prop in a simple snug bubble around its outside — fast, and good for most furniture. Off makes the barrier follow the prop's exact shape, holes and all — slower, but precise, for props with gaps you need characters to walk through.

How to place and edit props

  1. Switch to Object mode — click the Object button or press O. The Objects tab opens by itself.
  2. Stay in Paint sub-mode, pick a category and a prop, set the Snap, Rot and Brush as you like.
  3. Left-click in the 3D view to drop the prop. Right-click to erase props under your cursor (the Brush size decides how wide the erase reaches).
  4. To rearrange what's already there, click Select. Now left-click a prop to grab it, or drag a box across several to grab a bunch. Drag a grabbed prop to move it — and remember, if the Snap box is ticked, that move jumps to the building-block grid, just like placing does. Untick Snap to slide it anywhere freely.
    What you'll see

    The Selection section appears in the panel with rotation, height and collision controls for exactly what you grabbed. Change those numbers and the prop updates live.

  5. Use these keys right in the 3D view while props are selected:
    • Hold Shift and left-click a prop to add it to (or remove it from) your selection — handy for grabbing a few specific props that aren't side by side.
    • Q and E turn the selected prop a touch (a small step each press) — Q one way, E the other.
    • R randomizes the prop's turn — a quick way to make a row of plants or crates look natural instead of identical.
    • X or Delete removes the selected props.
    • Esc (or a right-click) lets go of everything, clearing your selection.

The Footstep tab — what your feet sound like

This tab paints an invisible sound coat onto your floor tiles. Walk on a tile painted "wood" and you hear footsteps on wood; walk on "water" and you hear splashes. The floor surface is locked flat here automatically — you're only ever painting floors.

Footstep tab controls
Material:
A label. Read-only.
Material list
A list of footstep sounds — cliff, dirt, tall grass, wood, water, road — each shown in its own color. Click one to make it your "brush."
Hint line
A read-only reminder: "LMB: Paint | RMB: Erase."
  1. Switch to Footstep mode — click the Footstep button or press T.
  2. Click a material in the list to pick the sound you want to paint.
  3. Left-click (or drag) over floor tiles to paint that sound on. Right-click to reset a tile back to the room's default sound.
    What you'll see

    While you're in Footstep mode, every floor tile is tinted by its sound's color — not just the ones you've painted. Tiles you haven't touched yet show the color of the room's default sound (set on the canvas, "wood" to begin with). So the whole floor lights up in colors right away: don't think only your hand-painted tiles are colored — the untouched ones are simply showing the default. That's how you can see at a glance that the bridge is "wood," the path is "dirt," and the pond is "water."

The Architecture tab — walls, columns and stairs

Architecture pieces are ready-made building blocks — wall segments, columns, staircases — that snap neatly to the grid and turn in clean quarter-turns. The editor is clever about wall ends: when you lay a run of wall, it automatically swaps in the correct left and right end-cap pieces so corners look finished. Like Objects, this tab has Paint and Select sub-modes.

Architecture tab controls
Paint / Select sub-mode
Paint places and erases pieces, with a live ghost preview that shows exactly where the next piece lands. Select picks existing pieces to move, rotate or delete. Switching to Paint clears your selection; switching to Select hides the ghost.
Category (dropdown)
The broad group — for example Interiors or Exteriors. Picking one refills the groups below.

Why don't I see my own folder? Just like the props, these are exactly the building-block folders your project was set up to use. A new folder of building pieces you've made won't appear until it's been added to the project's setup — that's the only reason a folder you can see on disk might not show up here.
Group (dropdown)
The family of pieces — "wall," "column," "stairs," and so on. One group is special: stairs is the only family that counts toward where characters can walk, so the game knows you can climb them.
Variant (dropdown)
The exact style within the group — a plain wall, a windowed wall, a fancy column. (It shows "(base)" if there's only one.)

Some pieces come with extra hidden versions whose names end in "_L", "_R", or "_LOD1"/"_LOD2". Those are deliberately left out of this list — the editor uses them automatically (the L/R ones become the left and right end caps of your wall runs; the LOD ones are the simpler far-away versions). So if you peek in your building-block folder and spot files like "wall_L" and "wall_R" but they don't show up as variants here, that's expected and correct — those are the end caps the editor swaps in for you, and the L/R dropdown just below is how you'd override them.
L/R (dropdown) — Auto / Left / Right
Controls those automatic end caps. Leave it on Auto and the editor picks the right left/right cap for the ends of your wall runs. Force Left or Right to override. It's greyed out unless the piece you picked actually has those hidden "_L"/"_R" versions in its folder — if a piece has no matching left/right files, there's nothing to override, so the dropdown simply isn't available. It resets to Auto whenever you change the variant.
Rotation: / readout
A label plus a read-only number showing the current turn — 0, 90, 180 or 270 degrees.
Rot R
Spins the next piece a quarter-turn. Click it or press R. The ghost preview turns live so you can see exactly how it'll sit.
Grid Size:
How big each grid cell is for snapping building pieces and props. It goes as fine as 0.0625 (a sixteenth of a unit — very tight) all the way up to 4 (big, coarse cells), and it always moves in steps of 0.0625, so valid values are 0.0625, 0.125, 0.1875, 0.25, and so on. The default is 0.5. This is separate from your tile size, so your building blocks and your tiles can use different grids — and it's the same grid the Objects tab's Snap box uses, so changing it here also changes how props snap.
Bake Mesh
When the room is finished, this bundles all your building pieces and tiles into one lightweight piece the game can draw quickly. It also makes simpler stand-in versions that the game quietly swaps in for rooms that are far away, so the game stays smooth. A "finalize for performance" button — click it when you're done building.
Externalize Materials
Saves the room's surface looks — its colors and tile images — into one shared folder, so several rooms can point at the same look instead of each keeping its own copy. A housekeeping button for keeping the project tidy.
Hint line
A read-only reminder of your current clicks. Paint: "LMB: Place/Drag | RMB: Erase/Drag | R: Rotate." Select: "LMB: Select | Shift: Toggle / Q/E: Rotate / Del: Delete."
  1. Switch to Architecture mode — click the Architecture button or press A.
  2. Pick a category, group and variant, set your rotation (the Rot button or R) and Grid Size.
  3. Left-click to place a piece, or hold and drag to lay a whole run. Right-click (or right-drag) to erase.
    What you'll see

    A see-through teal ghost of the piece floats at the grid cell under your mouse, turned exactly how it'll land. As you drag a wall run, the editor automatically swaps the end pieces to the correct left/right caps so the corners look right.

  4. To rearrange, click Select, then work right in the 3D view:
    • Left-click a piece to grab it, or drag a box over several to grab a bunch.
    • Hold Shift and left-click a piece to add it to (or remove it from) your selection.
    • Drag a grabbed piece to move it — it re-snaps to the grid as it goes.
    • Q and E turn the selection a quarter-turn (one each way).
    • X or Delete removes the selected pieces; Esc (or a right-click) lets go of the selection.

How to build each thing

Paint a floor or wall

  1. Pick a tile from the sheet at the bottom of the Tiles tab (click one square).
  2. Choose your surface — press 1 for floor, 2 for a front wall, 3 for a side wall (or just aim your camera and let it auto-pick).
  3. Set the pose — rotate with R, mirror with H or J if needed.
  4. Left-click to stamp. Hold and drag to lay down many tiles in a stroke. Right-click anytime to erase. Hold Shift and drag to flood a rectangle (same as Fill).

Erase tiles

Press E (or click Erase), then left-click or drag over tiles to rub them out. They only vanish from the layer you're on. Right-click also erases. To wipe a whole layer in one shot, use the Clear button (one undo brings it back).

Fill a big area

Press F, click one corner of the area, drag to the opposite corner, and let go. The whole rectangle fills with your tile, repeating it to cover the space. Works on floors and walls alike.

Tilt or warp a surface (Vertex)

Press V. Little round handles appear on your tile surface's corner points. You have to get your mouse fairly close to a handle for it to light up — hover near one and it highlights; if nothing highlights, your mouse just isn't close enough yet, so nudge it nearer. Click a highlighted handle to grab it, and drag to move it — it slides in steps the size of your Snap setting. Hold Shift and click to grab several points at once.

Watch out: clicking in truly empty space (not on any handle) lets go of everything — it clears your whole selection. So if you click a hair to the side of a handle and it seems like the tool "did nothing," you most likely missed the handle and cleared your selection instead. Aim right at the dot and wait for it to light up before clicking.

Good to know

Vertex nudges aren't fully covered by undo yet, so move thoughtfully and save when you're happy with a shape.

Drop a column (Shape)

Press Q. In the Shape controls set Sides, Radius and Height, pick a tile for the surface, then click in the 3D view. A many-sided column appears at that spot, each face wrapped in your tile. Every column you drop can be undone.

Place furniture (Objects)

Covered above — press O, pick a prop in Paint sub-mode, left-click to place and right-click to erase. Switch to Select to move and adjust existing props.

Choose footstep sounds

Press T, click a sound in the list, left-click your floors to paint it on, right-click to reset.

Build with walls and stairs (Architecture)

Covered above — press A, pick a group and variant, rotate with R, left-click or drag to place, right-click to erase.

Tell the game where to walk and what's solid

The order matters for one of these. First lay your floors and stairs, then click Nav in the Tiles tab to mark out where characters can walk — Nav only finds floors that already exist, so clicking it on a bare room does nothing. Collision, on the other hand, you can click at any point to rebuild the solid ground that keeps characters from falling through or passing through walls. Both also refresh automatically every time you save the scene, so you rarely have to think about them.

Finish the room for performance

When the room is done, hop to the Architecture tab and click Bake Mesh to bundle everything into one lightweight piece the game can draw quickly (it even makes simpler stand-in versions for when the room is far away, so the game stays smooth). Then, optionally, click Externalize Materials to save the room's surface looks — its colors and tile images — into one shared folder so other rooms can point at the same look.

Two batch helpers in the Project menu

Up in the editor's Project > Tools menu, the Tiles editor adds two "do it everywhere at once" commands. You won't need these day-to-day, but they're there for tidying up the whole project in one click.

Project > Tools commands
Mahou Tiled: Rebuild Collision + Nav for All Scenes
Goes through every room in the project and refreshes both the solid ground and the where characters can walk info, then saves each one. A one-click refresh for the whole game.
Mahou Tiled: Bake Interiors (Collision + Nav)
Takes your stub indoor rooms and sets them up properly — sharing their look, marking walkable areas, adding solid ground, and scaffolding their camera zones. It skips rooms that are already set up, so it's safe to run.
Heads up

You may also spot a "Convert ORM to RTLight..." item in the same Tools menu. That belongs to a different, unrelated helper and has nothing to do with building rooms — you can ignore it while working on tiles.

The glowing helpers in the 3D view

Everything in the 3D viewport that isn't your actual room is a helper the editor draws just for you. They're never in the finished game — they only show up here so you can aim.

What the overlays mean
The white grid
Your work surface, drawn at its current depth, sized to your tiles (or your architecture grid in Object and Architecture modes). Toggle it with the Grid button or G.
Faint blue depth lines
Soft lines on the surfaces just in front of and behind the active one, so you can judge depth in 3D. Just a visual aid.
The ghost tile under your mouse
A live preview of exactly what you're about to stamp — the real picture, with your flips, multi-tile block size, and pixel offset all applied — snapped to the grid. Move your mouse and it follows.
The yellow lock marker
A little crosshair-and-diamond that appears while you hold S, sitting on the nearest tile corner your surface will snap to. Release S to lock there.
Colored floor tints (Footstep mode)
Every floor tile shaded by its footstep sound's color, so you can read your sound map at a glance.
The teal ghost (Architecture Paint)
A see-through teal preview of the building piece you're about to place, at the snapped cell with your chosen rotation.

The two deep tricks worth memorizing

Hold S — snap your surface to a tile you already placed

This is the trick that makes building in 3D feel effortless. While painting, erasing, or filling tiles, hold the S key and hover your mouse near a tile you've already laid down. A yellow lock marker jumps to the nearest tile corner (or to the room's center). Let go of S and your work surface snaps to line up perfectly with that tile — same direction, same depth. Now your next stamps sit flush against what's already there. It works just like Sprytile's surface snapping. (The typed Lock X/Y/Z + Go controls do the same thing by hand if you prefer numbers.)

One catch worth remembering: this hold-S snap only works in those three tile-stamping modes (Paint, Erase, Fill). In Shape, Vertex, Object and Architecture modes, holding S does nothing at all.

Push the surface deeper with the bracket keys

Press [ to shove your work surface one tile-step deeper, and ] to bring it one step back. The plane readout updates so you always know how deep you are. This is how you build a second floor above the first, or a back wall behind a front one, without moving your camera.

Bring your own tiles, parts & props

The tile editor builds rooms and buildings from three kinds of art you supply: flat tile sheets (the painted surfaces), 3D modular parts (snap-together pieces you assemble walls, roofs and dungeons from), and 3D props (furniture and objects). Like terrain, you drop files into folders and a small settings file points the editor at them — no code.

Real files · You're Gonna Be Famous

Examples below are actual files from the flagship game. The folder paths are this game's layout; the shape and naming of each file is the part to copy.

Tile sheets

TILE SHEET · a grid of equal squarestiny example: 1 × 6 of 16px

A sheet is just equal squares in a grid. This tiny one is 6 tiles; real sheets are big grids (e.g. 640 × 1280 = a 40 × 80 grid).

You tell the editor the tile size (e.g. 16 pixels); then the sheet is read as a grid — width ÷ tile-size across, height ÷ tile-size down — so a 640 × 1280 picture at 16px is 40 × 80 tiles. Each tile you stamp can lie flat (a floor or ceiling) or stand upright (a front or side wall), and can be rotated or flipped. Save sheets in res://rooms_models/ and list the folder in the settings file tileset_directories.json (or pick one straight from the side panel).

Modular parts — snap-together pieces for exteriors, interiors & dungeons

This is the powerful one. The Architecture tab stamps 3D parts onto a grid, so you build a whole building, room, or dungeon out of reusable snap-together pieces — walls, roofs, doorways, columns, stairs. The same kit of parts can build a town exterior, a cosy interior, or a dungeon corridor; you stamp pieces on the grid, rotate them in quarter-turns, and the editor lines them up.

A part is a 3D model file (.glb) named GROUP_VARIANT, with two optional tails:

How a part's name works
GROUP the kit
Everything sharing a group name is one family of matching pieces (e.g. all the 225 pieces of one building style). The editor lists a kit together so its pieces fit each other.
_VARIANT the piece
Which piece it is within the kit — e.g. 225_roof_APEX1, 225_wall, 225_corner.
_L / _R mirror
Left- and right-facing versions of a piece (for corners and one-sided bits). The editor offers an Auto / Left / Right choice and flips to match.
_LOD1 … _LOD4 distance
Optional simpler versions shown far away to keep big scenes fast. Leave them out and the full piece is always used.

Real examples: simpleDoor.glb (a standalone piece) and 225_roof_APEX1.glb with its mirror 225_roof_APEX1_L.glb. Place them with the Architecture tab: pieces snap to the grid, rotate in 90° steps, and flip Left/Right as needed. Save outdoor kits in res://rooms_models/architecture/exteriors/ and indoor / dungeon kits in …/architecture/interiors/.

Props & objects

Props are free-standing 3D objects — furniture, a mailbox, a lamp — placed anywhere with any rotation (not locked to the grid). They're plain .glb files with no special naming. Save in res://rooms_models/props/interiors/ or …/exteriors/.

The naming cheat-sheet

Filename tails the editor understands
GROUP_VARIANT
Architecture pieces sharing a GROUP form one snap-together kit.
_L / _R
Mirror pair — left/right versions of one piece (parts and fences).
_LOD1…4
Simpler far-away versions (optional).
shared base name
Files sharing a base (e.g. fence, fence_L, fence_2) bundle into one family.

Keyboard shortcuts — the complete list

Every shortcut and every hold-a-key trick, in one place. These work while your mouse is over the 3D view.

Switch modes

  • B — Paint mode
  • E — Erase mode (but in Object mode, E turns the selected prop instead — see below)
  • F — Fill mode
  • V — Vertex mode
  • Q — Shape mode (but in Object or Architecture mode, Q turns the selection instead)
  • O — Object mode
  • T — Footstep mode
  • A — Architecture mode

Aim the work surface

  • 1 — Lock to floor (press again to release back to auto)
  • 2 — Lock to front/back wall (press again to release)
  • 3 — Lock to side wall (press again to release)
  • [ — Push the surface one step deeper
  • ] — Pull the surface one step back
  • Hold S — Show the yellow snap marker at the nearest tile corner (works only in Paint, Erase and Fill — does nothing in Shape, Vertex, Object or Architecture)
  • Release S — Lock the surface onto that marked corner

Pose the stamp

  • R — Rotate the stamp a quarter-turn (in Architecture Paint it turns the next piece; in Object Select it randomizes the prop's turn; in Architecture Select it turns the selection)
  • H — Mirror the stamp left-to-right
  • J — Mirror the stamp top-to-bottom

The view

  • G — Show or hide the grid overlay

Mouse, in every mode

  • Left-click / drag — Paint, fill, place a prop, place a piece, or select-and-drag (depending on mode)
  • Right-click — Erase (tiles, footstep, props, pieces) — or, in a Select sub-mode, let go of your selection
  • Hold Shift + left-click — In Paint mode, flood a rectangle; in a Select sub-mode, add or remove the nearest item from your selection; in Vertex, add or remove a point from your selection
  • Left-drag over empty space (in a Select sub-mode) — Draw a box to select many props or pieces at once

While objects or architecture are selected

In Object or Architecture mode these keys talk to your selection first, which is why they behave differently here than elsewhere:

  • Q — Object: turn the selected prop a touch counter-clockwise. Architecture: turn the selection a quarter-turn counter-clockwise.
  • E — Object: turn the selected prop a touch clockwise. Architecture: turn the selection a quarter-turn clockwise.
  • R — Object: randomize the prop's turn. Architecture Paint: cycle the next piece's rotation.
  • X or Delete — Remove the selected props or pieces
  • Escape — Let go of everything (clear the selection)

On the tile sheet (in the panel)

  • Left-click a square — Pick one tile
  • Left-click and drag across squares — Grab a rectangular block of tiles for a bigger stamp
The one gotcha

While you're in Object or Architecture mode, the keys Q, E, R, X, Delete and Escape act on your selection first — they won't jump you to another mode. So if you press Q expecting Shape mode but you're in Object mode, it'll rotate your prop instead. Switch out of Object/Architecture first if you want those keys to change modes.

Settings on the canvas itself

A handful of settings live on your Tiled Map canvas in the Inspector, mostly so you can set them once and forget them. The panel edits most of these for you, but here's what they mean.

Canvas settings
Tile sheet
The image full of tile pictures the room paints from. Set it here or pick it from the panel's Tileset dropdown.
Tile size
How big each little square is on the sheet (default is 16). Match this to your art.
Pixels per unit
How big a tile ends up in the actual world (default 32). This sets the real-world size of every tile.
Default footstep sound
The sound a floor makes if you never painted a specific one onto it (default is "wood"). The Footstep tab edits this.
Generate solid ground (on/off)
Whether the room keeps its solid ground up to date. Leave it on so characters can stand on your floors.
Architecture grid size
The snap-grid for building pieces and props (default 0.5). The Architecture tab's Grid Size spinner edits this.

A quick start, end to end

  1. Select your Tiled Map canvas so the panel and grid appear.
  2. Pick a tile from the sheet, press 1 for the floor, and left-drag to paint a floor — or press F and drag a rectangle to fill it fast.
  3. Press 2 or 3 and paint the walls. Hold S near a floor edge and release to line a wall up perfectly.
  4. Press A and snap in some doorways, columns or stairs; press O and scatter in furniture.
    Heads up

    The moment you press O or A, the letter keys Q, E and R change jobs — they now turn and randomize your selected props/pieces instead of switching modes. So if you press Q here hoping for Shape mode, you'll rotate a prop instead. See "The one gotcha" callout in the shortcuts section for the full story.

  5. Press T and paint footstep sounds onto the floors.
  6. Click Nav and Collision in the Tiles tab so the game knows where characters can walk and what's solid.
  7. Click Bake Mesh in the Architecture tab to finalize the room. Done!
